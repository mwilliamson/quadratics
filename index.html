Quadratic equations

Introduction

Linear equations are made up of two different types of terms:

  • Variables, such as $$ x $$. They might be multiplied by a number e.g. $$ -2x $$, $$ \frac{3}{4} x $$.
  • Constants, such as $$ 6 $$, $$ -19 $$, or $$ \frac{1}{2} $$. In other words, a single number.

By putting these pieces together, we can make linear equations:

$$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{split} 2x + 4 & = 0 \\ 3x - 9 & = 16 \\ 6x - 4 & = x + 2 \\ -4x & = 5 \\ \end{split} \end{equation*} $$$

Quadratic equations are made up of the same terms as linear equations, plus one more: a variable squared, such as $$x^2$$. Using this extra piece, we can make quadratic equations:

$$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{split} x^2 + 3x + 2 & = 0 \\ x^2 - 4x & = 12 \\ 4x^2 & = 0 \\ -3x^2 + 27 & = 54 \\ \end{split} \end{equation*} $$$

Visualising quadratic equations

When we have a linear equation, we can visualise it by plotting in a graph. We do this by the following steps:

  1. Rearrange the equation so that one side of the equation is $$ 0 $$
  2. Replace the $$ 0 $$ with $$ y $$
