Introduction

Linear equations are made up of two different types of terms:

  • Variables, such as $$ x $$. They might be multiplied by a number e.g. $$ -2x $$, $$ \frac{3}{4} x $$.
  • Constants, such as $$ 6 $$, $$ -19 $$, or $$ \frac{1}{2} $$. In other words, a single number.

By putting these pieces together, we can make linear equations:

$$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{split} 2x + 4 & = 0 \\ 3x - 9 & = 16 \\ 6x - 4 & = x + 2 \\ -4x & = 5 \\ \end{split} \end{equation*} $$$

Quadratic equations are made up of the same terms as linear equations, plus one more: a variable squared, such as $$x^2$$. Using this extra piece, we can make quadratic equations:

$$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{split} x^2 + 3x + 2 & = 0 \\ x^2 - 4x & = 12 \\ 4x^2 & = 0 \\ -3x^2 + 27 & = 54 \\ \end{split} \end{equation*} $$$

Visualising quadratic equations

We can visualise linear equations by plotting one side of the equation on a graph. For instance, if we have $$ 2x + 4 = 5 - 3x $$, we can visualise the left-hand side by plotting $$ y = 2x + 4 $$.

If we plot both sides of a linear equation, then the solution for $$ x $$ is the $$ x $$ coordinate where the lines meet. For instance, if we have the equation $$ 2x + 4 = 5 - 3x $$, we can plot $$ y = 2x + 4 $$ and $$ y = 5 - 3x $$.

To makes things simpler, we normally rearrange linear equations so that one side is zero. For instance, if we have the equation $$ 2x + 4 = 5 - 3x $$, we can rearrange it to $$ 5x - 1 = 0 $$. As before, we can plot both sides of the equation, with the solution being the x coordinate where the lines meet. Since one side of the equation is zero, one of the lines we plot is $$ y = 0 $$, which is the same as the x-axis. Therefore, our solution is the $$ x $$ coordinate where $$ y = 5x - 1 $$ meets the x-axis.

In general, if we rearrange an equation so that one of the sides is zero, the solution is the $$ x $$ coordinate where the left-hand side meets the x-axis.
